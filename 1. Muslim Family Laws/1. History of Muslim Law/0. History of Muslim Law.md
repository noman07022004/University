#### **1Ô∏è‚É£ 1.1: Pre-Islamic Background**

- **Overview**: The pre-Islamic period, known as _Jahiliyyah_ (Age of Ignorance), was characterized by a tribal, patriarchal society in Arabia where unwritten customs governed social and familial relations. These practices often marginalized women and prioritized tribal honor over individual rights.
- **Key Features**:
    - **Social Structure**: Arabian society was organized into tribes, with loyalty to the tribe superseding individual justice. Disputes were resolved by tribal leaders based on custom, often leading to blood feuds.
    - **Marriage Practices**: Polygamy was unrestricted, with no formal requirements for dower (mahr) or consent. Temporary marriages and marriage by capture were common, and women had little agency.
    - **Status of Women**: Women were frequently denied inheritance and property rights. Female infanticide was practiced in some tribes due to economic burdens or preference for male heirs.
    - **Inheritance**: Property passed primarily to male heirs, with women and minors often excluded. No codified system existed, leading to arbitrary distributions.
- **Reforms by Islam**: Islamic law, introduced later, revolutionized these practices by mandating dower (Quran 4:4), consent in marriage, and inheritance rights for women (Quran 4:11-12), laying the foundation for equitable family law.
- **Relevance to Bangladesh**:
    - Understanding pre-Islamic practices highlights the transformative impact of Islamic law, which forms the basis for modern statutes like the _Muslim Personal Law (Shariat) Application Act, 1937_, ensuring women‚Äôs rights to inheritance and dower.
    - Family courts in Bangladesh refer to these reforms when resolving disputes, e.g., ensuring daughters receive Quranic inheritance shares.
- **Significance**: Provides the baseline for appreciating Islamic law‚Äôs reforms, essential for course learning outcome 1 (knowing sources and applicability).

#### **2Ô∏è‚É£ 1.2: Muhammad and the Quran**

- **Overview**: This subtopic focuses on Prophet Muhammad‚Äôs role as the final messenger of Islam and the Quran‚Äôs introduction as the primary source of legal principles for family matters, marking the advent of Islamic law in the early 7th century CE.
- **Key Features**:
    - **Prophet‚Äôs Role**: Muhammad received the Quran over 23 years (610‚Äì632 CE), conveying its legal and moral guidance. His Sunnah (practices, sayings, approvals) provided practical applications, e.g., marriage contracts with dower and consent.
    - **Quranic Provisions**:
        - **Marriage**: Mandated dower (Quran 4:4) and consent, prohibiting forced marriages (Quran 4:19).
        - **Divorce**: Regulated talaq, iddat (waiting period), and reconciliation (Quran 2:229‚Äì231).
        - **Inheritance**: Specified fixed shares for heirs, including women (Quran 4:11-12), ensuring equitable distribution.
    - **Reforms**: The Quran abolished female infanticide, limited polygamy to four wives with fairness (Quran 4:3), and granted women legal rights absent in pre-Islamic Arabia.
- **Relevance to Bangladesh**:
    - The Quran and Sunnah are primary sources for statutes like the _Muslim Family Laws Ordinance, 1961_, which codifies dower and divorce procedures.
    - Family courts rely on Quranic verses and Prophetic practices to resolve disputes, e.g., determining valid marriage contracts or talaq conditions.
- **Significance**: Establishes the divine foundation of Muslim law, critical for course learning outcome 1 and underpinning all subsequent legal developments.

#### **3Ô∏è‚É£ 1.3: First Century of Quran**

- **Overview**: Spanning 632‚Äì718 CE, this period under the Rightly Guided Caliphs (Abu Bakr, Umar, Uthman, Ali) saw the initial application of Quranic family laws, marking the transition from revelation to practice.
- **Key Features**:
    - **Caliphal Leadership**: Caliphs acted as judges, enforcing Quranic rules on marriage, divorce, and inheritance. Umar standardized dower amounts to prevent exploitation.
    - **Expansion Challenges**: Islam‚Äôs spread to Syria, Iraq, and Egypt required adapting Quranic laws to diverse cultures, sparking early interpretive debates.
    - **Early Scholarship**: Companions like Ali and Ibn Abbas interpreted laws, laying the groundwork for fiqh (jurisprudence), e.g., clarifying inheritance for complex families.
- **Relevance to Bangladesh**:
    - Early practices inform the Hanafi school, shaping statutes like the _Dissolution of Muslim Marriages Act, 1939_, which incorporates Quranic divorce principles.
    - Family courts draw on these precedents for consistent rulings, e.g., enforcing iddat periods.
- **Significance**: Demonstrates the practical implementation of Quranic law, aligning with course learning outcome 3 (application of sources).

#### **4Ô∏è‚É£ 1.4: Umayyad Administration**

- **Overview**: The Umayyad Caliphate (661‚Äì750 CE) centralized legal administration, with qadis enforcing family law across a vast empire, though political influences sparked opposition.
- **Key Features**:
    - **Centralized Governance**: Qadis appointed by caliphs standardized marriage, divorce, and inheritance rules based on Quran and Sunnah.
    - **Early Codification**: Documented rulings ensured consistency, e.g., inheritance shares (Quran 4:11-12).
    - **Challenges**: Political appointments of qadis led to accusations of secularizing law, prompting scholarly opposition.
- **Relevance to Bangladesh**:
    - The Umayyad model of state-enforced law influenced later Islamic governance, informing modern statutes like the _Family Courts Ordinance, 1985_.
    - Family courts reflect this centralized approach by applying statutory laws uniformly.
- **Significance**: Highlights the institutionalization of family law, relevant for understanding state-driven legal systems.

#### **5Ô∏è‚É£ 1.5: Ancient Schools of Law**

- **Overview**: The 8th‚Äì9th centuries saw the emergence of Hanafi, Maliki, Shafi‚Äôi, and Hanbali schools, formalizing family law through distinct jurisprudential methodologies.
- **Key Features**:
    - **Hanafi School**: Founded by Abu Hanifa, emphasized reasoning (ra‚Äôy) and qiyas, allowing flexible marriage contracts and divorce rulings.
    - **Maliki School**: Prioritized Medina‚Äôs practices, strict on divorce procedures.
    - **Shafi‚Äôi School**: Systematized _usul al-fiqh_, balancing Quran, Sunnah, ijma, and qiyas.
    - **Hanbali School**: Emphasized textual literalism, conservative on family law.
- **Relevance to Bangladesh**:
    - The Hanafi school‚Äôs dominance shapes statutes like the _Muslim Personal Law (Shariat) Application Act, 1937_, allowing stipulations in marriage contracts.

- **Significance**: Critical for understanding fiqh‚Äôs development, aligning with course learning outcome 1.

#### **6Ô∏è‚É£ 1.6: Opposition Movements and the Traditionists**

- **Overview**: Opposition to Umayyad deviations led to movements emphasizing authentic hadith, with traditionists like Imam Bukhari compiling reliable collections.
- **Key Features**:
    - **Traditionists‚Äô Role**: Verified hadith clarified family law, e.g., dower (Quran 4:4) and iddat (Quran 2:228).

- **Relevance to Bangladesh**:

- **Significance**: Strengthens the textual basis of family law, relevant for course learning outcome 3.

- **Key Features**:

    - **Shafi‚Äôi‚Äôs Contribution**: His _Al-Risala_ formalized legal methodology, ensuring uniformity.
